# An Architecture for the Grey Man
## Building the Republic After the Fictions Fall

## I. The Debt the Previous Essays Created

Three essays have now been spent on demolition. The presidency was named as a structural error inherited from monarchy. Democracy was named as a fallacy in which the form of consent has been substituted for the substance of self-government. Power itself was named as a fiction, a word that lodged a relational pattern in individuals and made civilization fight, for millennia, over a substance that does not exist.

Demolition is the easy part. It is also, in the end, the irresponsible part — if the demolisher leaves without rebuilding. There is a kind of writer who specializes in elegant critique and never owes the reader an alternative, and that writer is, in his own way, part of the problem he criticizes. The critic who refuses to build leaves his readers in worse shape than he found them: convinced of the failure of what exists, with nothing to replace it, vulnerable to whichever charlatan arrives first with a confident answer.

This essay is the answer to the debt. Not the only possible answer — there will be others, better than this one, written by others — but a serious one, built from the materials we have, designed for the world we actually inhabit. It is the architecture that the previous critiques imply, made specific enough to be tested, criticized, improved, and ultimately built.

The architecture has a name in the broader project this essay belongs to. It is called ¡BASTA![^1], which is what an exhausted population finally says when it stops waiting to be saved. But the name is not what matters. What matters is the structure.

One terminological note before continuing. The previous essay argued that power is a permission, not a substance. The architecture this essay describes therefore defends against the *concentration of permissions* — not the seizure of any thing. The vocabulary that follows (capture, anti-capture, distribution) refers to that, in this corrected sense. The danger is the same; the noun has only been clarified.

## II. What an Architecture Has to Do

Before describing the architecture itself, we have to be honest about what any post-presidential, post-electoral, post-power-fiction structure must accomplish. Five requirements. Get any of them wrong and the whole thing collapses back into the pathologies of what came before.

**It has to coordinate at scale without concentration.** The fundamental problem of human cooperation in groups larger than a village is that face-to-face deliberation breaks down. The traditional solution was hierarchy: a few decide for the many. The modern solution must be different — coordination through protocol rather than command, through architecture rather than authority. The architecture must allow millions of people to act coherently without anyone being in charge of the millions.

**It has to be capture-resistant by design.** Every previous architecture for human cooperation has been captured, eventually, by some combination of donors, charismatic individuals, foreign interests, professional political classes, or entrenched bureaucracies. An architecture that does not assume capture as its default state will be captured. The defense cannot be vigilance, because vigilance fails. The defense has to be structural — built into the protocols themselves, automatic, difficult to circumvent without breaking the system in ways that everyone can see.

**It has to be legitimate without being charismatic.** The previous architectures derived their legitimacy from people: kings who claimed divine right, presidents who claimed popular mandate, founders who claimed wisdom. Each of these legitimacy structures collapses when the person fails or the people stop believing. A durable architecture has to derive legitimacy from process, transparency, and demonstrable function — not from any individual's mystique. It has to work whether the people running it are admirable or ordinary, because over time most of them will be ordinary.

**It has to be participatory without being exhausting.** A common failure of well-intentioned alternative governance is that it demands so much of the citizen that only the obsessed can participate. This produces, over time, a class of professional participants whose interests diverge from the public's — exactly the pathology the architecture was supposed to escape. The architecture must demand of the ordinary citizen only what an ordinary citizen can give: attention when it matters, deliberation when invited, contribution where natural, and the option of more involvement without the requirement of it.

**It has to evolve without dissolving.** Every governance architecture eventually faces conditions its designers did not anticipate. The architecture must be amendable from within, by transparent process, on timelines that allow real deliberation. It must also be defensible against amendments that would compromise its core anti-capture principles. The hardest engineering problem in any constitutional system is distinguishing between healthy evolution and slow capture. The architecture must build that distinction into its structure.

These five requirements — coordination, capture-resistance, legitimacy, participation, evolution — define the design space. Any architecture worth building must satisfy all of them. Most existing architectures satisfy two or three.

## III. The Five Layers

The architecture proposed here has five layers, each addressing a different scale of human coordination. They are not levels of a hierarchy. They are layers of a stack, in the technical sense — each providing services to the layer above and depending on the layer below, with clean interfaces between them.

**Layer One: The Neighborhood.** The smallest unit of civic life, where face-to-face deliberation is still possible. A neighborhood in this architecture is not a place where people happen to live near each other; it is a defined civic body of perhaps fifty to five hundred households, with the authority and the resources to make real decisions about what concerns its members directly: local infrastructure, public space, neighborhood-scale services, the small arbitrations that occupy most of daily political life. The neighborhood is the layer where most citizens, most of the time, do their actual self-government.

**Layer Two: The Commune or District.** A coordinating body of perhaps twenty to a hundred neighborhoods, handling questions that exceed neighborhood scale but remain local: schools, primary healthcare, transportation within the district, environmental management of shared resources. The commune is composed of representatives from each neighborhood, but not in the conventional sense. They are delegates with specific mandates, recallable at any time, rotating on short cycles, and accompanied at the commune level by citizens selected by sortition, so that no professional class of district politicians can form.

**Layer Three: The Region.** A larger coordinating body for what we currently call provincial or state-level concerns: secondary infrastructure, regional economic coordination, cultural and educational policy at scale, environmental management of larger systems. The region is where the architecture begins to require formal protocols rather than face-to-face deliberation, and where the design becomes most demanding. Capture risk is highest at this layer, and the anti-capture mechanisms must be most rigorous.

**Layer Four: The Federation.** What we currently call national government, but reconceived. Not a sovereign state with a chief executive, but a federation of regions coordinating on the matters that genuinely require national-scale action: defense, currency, fundamental rights, treaties, infrastructure of national scope. The federation has no president. Its executive function is held by a council, rotated, transparent, and stripped of the messianic costume that the presidency wore. Its legislative function combines elected representatives, sortition-selected citizens, and specialized deliberative bodies for technical questions. Its judicial function is independent and designed to be slow, deliberate, and protective of the architecture's core principles.

**Layer Five: The Civilizational Layer.** What lies beyond the federation: the international, the planetary, the questions that no single country can address. The same five requirements scale up — they have to — and three principles extend with particular force at this layer. Coordination without concentration: the international layer is a federation of federations, not a global hierarchy with a chief at the top; the architecture refuses, at this scale as at every other, the consolidation of permissions in any single body. Radical transparency between nations: the flows that matter at the civilizational scale — capital, arms, climate effects, data, displaced populations — must be visible to the citizens of every country they touch, because no nation can govern itself when the consequential decisions about its conditions are made invisibly elsewhere. Fork-rights between nations: the right of a country to exit, to reconstitute, to refuse a treaty whose terms have stopped serving its citizens, with transparent procedure and at real cost — the same principle that protects the neighborhood from the commune, the commune from the federation, must protect the federation from the civilizational order it participates in.

These five layers are not new in their components — most have antecedents in some functioning system somewhere. What is new is the integration: the explicit design of the stack as a stack, with clean interfaces, capture-resistance built in at every layer, and the recognition that the architecture's job is not to produce great leaders but to make great leadership unnecessary.

## IV. The Three Roles

Within this architecture, every position of civic responsibility falls into one of three roles. The roles are designed so that their incentives, term limits, and accountability mechanisms differ — making capture of all three by any single faction structurally extremely difficult.

**The Delegate.** Elected by their neighborhood, commune, or region, with a specific mandate. Term limits are short — typically one to three years, never renewable consecutively, with a hard lifetime cap on years served in any delegate role. Delegates are recallable at any time by the body that elected them, through transparent procedure. Their job is to represent the explicit, deliberated positions of the people who sent them, not their own judgment. When the people who sent them change their minds, the delegate's mandate changes. This is the role most similar to existing elected office, but stripped of the discretion and the longevity that allow capture.

**The Sortition Citizen.** Selected by lottery from the eligible population, serving a defined term, given the resources, time, and expert support to deliberate seriously on the questions before them. Sortition citizens sit alongside delegates at every layer above the neighborhood, ensuring that ordinary citizens — not professional political actors — are present in every meaningful deliberation. They cannot be reelected, because they were never elected. They are far harder to capture by donors, because they did not need donors to arrive. They cannot become a class, because they rotate. The presence of sortition citizens at every layer is one of the architecture's strongest anti-capture mechanisms.

**The Steward.** A specialized role for the long-term care of specific functions: the defense of fundamental rights, the maintenance of critical infrastructure, the curation of cultural and educational continuity, the protection of ecological systems. Stewards serve longer terms than delegates, because the work requires it, but they are constrained by transparent mandates, regular review, and hard term limits. Stewards are selected by mixed processes — partly elected, partly chosen by sortition, partly by demonstrated competence in the relevant field. Their authority is custodial, not proprietary. They hold something in trust on behalf of others, with the explicit expectation that they will hand it on.

Three roles, three different selection mechanisms, three different term structures, three different accountability paths. The architecture is designed so that no single faction can dominate all three simultaneously. Donors can capture some delegates; sortition is much harder to reach. Charismatic leaders can mobilize voters; they cannot bypass the deliberative architecture in which decisions are actually made. Bureaucracies can entrench themselves in steward roles; they are checked by delegates and sortition citizens at every decision point.

The architecture does not eliminate the possibility of capture. It makes capture expensive, visible, and resistible. That is the realistic standard. Anything more ambitious is utopian, and utopias get captured faster than realistic systems do.

## V. The Civic Capability Stack

An architecture is only the skeleton. The body requires capabilities — the actual capacities that allow citizens to participate, deliberate, decide, and remember. The architecture proposed here depends on a civic capability stack with six layers, each of which has to be built and maintained as conscious infrastructure, owned by the citizens who use it, governed by the citizens who use it, capture-resistant by protocol design and not by promise.

**Identity.** The architecture requires that citizens be identifiable as citizens — but the identity has to be a key the citizen carries, never a permission a platform grants. Cryptographically verifiable. Citizen-controlled. Separable across contexts. Auditable in aggregate, private in particulars. Jurisdictionally portable, so that no foreign company and no future government can revoke a person's standing in the country whose citizen she is. The vault is hers. The key is hers. The protocol that issues the key is built and governed by the citizenry, not contracted from a vendor whose terms can change without consultation.

**Deliberation.** The architecture requires infrastructure for serious, structured, evidence-informed deliberation among citizens at every layer. Not online comment sections. Not town halls dominated by the loudest voice. Not feeds engineered to maximize engagement at the cost of every other quality. Real deliberative processes that hold long argument, surface dissent rather than burying it, age well rather than degrading into spectacle, and reward thinking rather than reaction. The infrastructure for this kind of deliberation does not exist by default. It has to be built, by citizens for citizens, with the deliberate refusal of the metrics that have made the existing platforms unfit for civic life.

**Decision.** When deliberation produces a decision, the architecture requires mechanisms for that decision to be implemented, monitored, and adjusted. Sortition for the bodies that decide; liquid delegation for the voice that flows; participatory budgeting at the scale of the neighborhood; transparent budgeting at the scale of the federation; clear accountability for execution; public review of outcomes; the capacity to revise decisions when reality refuses to cooperate. The decision tooling has to be citizen-runnable, not platform-mediated. Most existing democracies fail catastrophically at the decision-implementation interface: legislatures pass laws that bureaucracies ignore, executives sign decrees that courts reverse, and the citizen has no idea who is responsible for any particular outcome. The architecture treats the interface as a first-class problem and designs it.

**Memory.** The architecture requires that decisions, deliberations, and their outcomes be preserved as civic memory, accessible to future citizens, structured so that the question *how did we get here* has a real answer. Civic memory has to be held in archives that no future government, no future quarterly board, and no future war can revoke. Federated. Held in many hands. Resistant to deletion as a property of the protocol, not of any current administrator's good intentions. Most political failures are repetitions of past failures, by populations that have forgotten what was tried before. The country whose memory belongs to a vendor is a country whose past can be edited.

**Communication.** The architecture requires media systems that serve civic deliberation rather than capture attention for advertisers. Federated, citizen-owned, free of recommendation algorithms tuned for outrage. Speech that survives without rewarding rage. This is the layer of the stack most degraded in current democracies, and the most consequential. Without accurate, accessible, non-capture-prone media, no other layer of the stack works. The reconstruction of civic media — through public infrastructure, cooperative ownership, protocol-level interventions, and the cultivation of citizen literacy — is one of the central tasks the architecture demands.

**Education.** The capacity to participate is not innate. It has to be cultivated. The architecture requires educational systems that prepare citizens for self-government — not merely for employment, not merely for consumption, but for the actual practice of deliberation, decision, stewardship, and contribution. This means civics taught as a craft rather than as a memorization of constitutional trivia. It means schools designed as small republics where children learn participation by practicing it. It means lifelong civic education for adults, woven into the architecture itself, with materials open and revisable by the citizens whose children will study them.

Six layers — identity, deliberation, decision, memory, communication, education. Each one is itself a major undertaking. Together, they constitute the civic capability stack on which the architecture depends. A country that has the architecture without the stack has only the appearance of self-government. A country that has the stack without the architecture has the capacity for self-government without the structures that channel it. Both are needed. Both have to be built, in parallel, by the same generation.

A note on urgency, sober rather than panicked. The capabilities described above are not optional add-ons to be assembled at leisure. The world already contains identity systems, deliberation platforms, decision tools, memory archives, media systems, and educational infrastructures. They have been built by actors whose interests are not the citizenry's. Most of what mediates civic life today is captured, by default, because it was built by people whose business model required capture. The capabilities the architecture depends on therefore have to be built as alternatives — sovereign, accountable, capture-resistant by protocol design — and they have to be built now, while the citizenry still has the technical literacy, the cultural memory, and the institutional space to build them. Citizens who build now set the architecture of the next century. Citizens who wait inherit what others built during the wait. The work is not heroic. Some of it is forking what is already working elsewhere; some of it is federating what already exists; some of it is invention. All of it is the same labor: claiming the civic capability stack as ours, before it is permanently claimed by interests for whom an inert citizenry is the equilibrium they prefer.

## VI. The Anti-Capture Mechanisms

Capture is the central enemy. Every governance architecture in human history has eventually been captured by some combination of forces with concentrated interests in subverting it. The architecture proposed here treats capture-resistance as the highest design priority, second only to functioning at all.

**Hard term limits, lifetime caps.** No one serves for life. No one accumulates decades of incumbency. The total time any citizen can spend in delegate or steward roles is sharply capped, ensuring that no professional political class can form. This is non-negotiable and constitutionally entrenched.

**Sortition at every layer.** As described above, the presence of sortition-selected citizens at every meaningful deliberation makes capture by any single faction enormously more expensive. You can buy delegates; you cannot buy your way into a lottery.

**Radical transparency.** Every decision, every flow of money near power, every meeting of consequence, every vote of every delegate and steward, is public by default. Privacy exists for citizens, not for officeholders. The asymmetry is intentional. Anyone exercising civic authority does so in full view, all the time.

**Fork rights.** The most distinctive anti-capture mechanism. Communities, regions, even neighborhoods retain the right to fork — to exit the larger architecture and reconstitute themselves under modified terms — through transparent procedure. This is the structural equivalent of an exit door. Architectures with no exit eventually become prisons. The fork right ensures that the architecture has to keep earning its members' participation, generation by generation. It cannot rely on lock-in.

**Sunset clauses on every authority.** No agency, no law, no mandate is permanent. Every authority granted by the architecture expires unless renewed, by deliberative process, on a transparent schedule. This forces continuous reexamination of why each piece of the structure exists, and prevents the accumulation of legacy bureaucracies that no one can remember authorizing.

**Mandatory rotation.** Within the structure, rotation is constant. Delegates rotate. Sortition citizens rotate. Stewards rotate. The composition of every body changes regularly, by design, so that no group of incumbents can entrench themselves.

**Independent audit.** Every layer of the architecture is audited by bodies independent of the layer being audited, with the audit reports published immediately and unredacted. The audit function is itself protected by sortition and rotation, so that the auditors cannot be captured either.

**Emergency limits.** The architecture explicitly limits what can be done in emergencies. Most democratic backsliding occurs through emergency powers that outlive the emergencies that justified them. The architecture sunsets emergency authority automatically and aggressively, with the burden of renewal placed on those who would extend it.

These eight mechanisms, working together, constitute the anti-capture infrastructure. None of them is novel in isolation. What is novel is treating capture-resistance as a design discipline, integrated through the whole architecture rather than appended to it.

## VII. The Phases of Construction

An architecture this ambitious cannot be installed by decree. It has to be built, in phases, by the people who will inhabit it. Four phases, each addressing a different challenge, each making the next one possible.

**Phase One: The Demonstration.** Small-scale instances of the architecture, built where they can be built — in neighborhoods, cooperatives, professional associations, civic platforms, online communities — that demonstrate the principles in working form. The point of the demonstration phase is not to govern anything large; it is to produce working examples that can be pointed at, studied, and replicated. Most political transformations begin with demonstrations that no one in power notices until it is too late.

**Phase Two: The Network.** The demonstrations connect. They share protocols, exchange lessons, federate where useful. The network phase is where the architecture begins to have its own scale, its own coherence, its own capacity to do things that no single demonstration could do alone. It is also where the architecture becomes visible to existing institutions, which respond with some combination of co-option, ignored, or hostility. The network has to be designed to survive all three.

**Phase Three: The Parallel Institution.** The network thickens into an institution that can perform real civic functions for its participants — handling disputes, coordinating resources, deliberating on shared questions, building infrastructure. The parallel institution does not replace the existing state. It exists alongside it, as a legitimate alternative for those who choose to participate. Citizens increasingly experience the parallel institution as more responsive, more transparent, and more genuinely theirs than the state, and the legitimacy gradient begins to shift.

**Phase Four: The Transition.** At some point, the legitimacy of the parallel institution exceeds that of the captured state, and a transition becomes possible. The transition can take many forms — gradual absorption, formal constitutional reform, peaceful displacement, or some combination. The architecture is designed so that the transition does not require revolution in the violent sense. It requires only that enough citizens conclude, on the basis of demonstrated function, that the parallel institution is the one they actually want to live under.

This is the long game. It is measured in decades, not election cycles. It is not glamorous. It does not produce charismatic leaders or televised victories. It produces, slowly, a different country, built underneath the existing one, until the existing one becomes a museum.

## VIII. The Argentine Mirror, Made Specific

Argentina is the country in which the previous essays were rooted, and it is the country in which this architecture is being built. The mirror, made specific, looks like this.

The country has spent forty-three years cycling through presidents, parties, and currencies, while the underlying fabric of cooperation has been allowed to fray. Each cycle has been more exhausted than the last. *La grieta* has consumed the energy that should have gone into building anything. Every faction has organized around the conquest of the state, and the state has hollowed out beneath them.

The architecture proposed here is what would replace the grieta with something worth fighting for. Neighborhoods that actually decide their own questions, with real resources and real authority. Communes that handle schools, healthcare, and local infrastructure without the paralysis of provincial politics. Regions that coordinate on what regions should coordinate on. A federation that does what a federation should do — defense, currency, fundamental rights, treaties — without the messianic costume of the presidency. Sortition citizens at every layer, ensuring that the grieta cannot capture the architecture the way it has captured every previous form. Stewards who hold what needs holding, on terms that prevent them from becoming the new entrenched class.

This is not a fantasy. The components exist. The neighborhood assemblies that emerged during the 2001 crisis demonstrated that Argentine citizens can, in fact, deliberate and decide together when given the chance. The cooperative tradition in Argentina is older and stronger than most people realize. The country has the literacy, the civic memory, the cultural depth, and the recent disillusionment necessary to build something better. What it has lacked is the architecture to channel those capacities into sustained, capture-resistant institutions.

The architecture proposed here is what the grey man has been waiting for, without knowing it. It is the answer to a question he has been asking, in his exhaustion and his frustration, for forty-three years. *If not this, then what?* The answer is: this, with you in it, building it where you stand, until what stands becomes the country.

## IX. What This Essay Has Not Solved

I want to be honest about what this architecture does not do, because no architecture solves everything, and the ones that pretend to are dangerous.

It does not solve the international layer. A country that builds this architecture still exists in a world of states, markets, and powers that operate by different logics. The architecture has to be defensible against those logics, not transcendent of them. That is harder than the architecture itself, and not the work of one essay or one country.

The minimum the architecture can owe at this scale is what Layer Five named: coordination without concentration, transparency between nations, fork-rights between nations. These are not solutions; they are the principles a country brings with it when it participates in the civilizational layer, so that what it built at home is not undone by what it accepts abroad.

It does not solve the question of violence. The architecture assumes a society in which most disputes are resolved by deliberation rather than by force. Real societies contain violence — criminal, political, structural. The architecture must include legitimate, accountable, capture-resistant capacities for the management of violence, and this essay has not specified them. That work is owed.

What can be said in advance: any legitimate use of force must be authorized by a body that contains delegates, sortition citizens, and stewards in the same proportions as the architecture's other deliberative bodies; must be subject to the same radical transparency and sunset discipline as every other authority; and must be exercised by personnel whose long-term loyalty is to the architecture rather than to any officeholder. A police or a military whose oath is to a person, a party, or a doctrine is not the architecture's police or military. The full design is owed; these are its non-negotiables.

It does not solve the question of speed. Deliberative architectures are slower than command architectures. There are crises in which speed matters more than wisdom. The architecture must be capable of acting quickly in emergencies without permanently distorting itself in the process. The mechanisms for emergency response need to be specified more precisely than space here permits.

The principle, at least, is sayable. Emergency authority is granted in narrow, time-bound, scope-bound packages by the deliberative bodies that hold ordinary authority, with automatic sunset clauses that require renewal under transparent procedure rather than tolerating extension by inertia. The history of democratic backsliding is the history of emergency powers that outlasted their emergencies. The architecture treats that as the failure mode to design against, not as the cost of being able to respond at all.

It does not solve the question of the first generation. An architecture designed for citizens who have been trained in self-government is being proposed for citizens who have, mostly, been trained in dependence. The transition demands not just structural change but cultural change, and culture changes on timelines that resist planning. The architecture has to be patient with itself, and so do the people building it.

These are not fatal limitations. They are the open questions that the next generation of work must address. The architecture proposed here is sufficient to begin. It is not sufficient to finish. No architecture ever is.

## X. The Beautiful Part

The grey man has been told, all his life, that politics is a contest of substances held by other people. The previous essays demolished that telling, layer by layer. This essay says: here is what is possible instead.

What is possible is a country in which the ordinary citizen actually governs the matters that affect his life, in collaboration with his neighbors, through structures designed so that no one — not even him, not even the people he agrees with — can capture the architecture and turn it against the rest. What is possible is a civic life in which contribution, influence, stewardship, and consent are the actual currencies, because the architecture is designed to recognize and channel them rather than to suppress them under the costume of power. What is possible is a republic that has dropped the king's costume entirely, and discovered that the body underneath was always the point.

This is not a utopia. The architecture proposed here will fail in specific ways. The first instances will be clumsy. The first generation of participants will make mistakes that later generations will laugh at. Some of the anti-capture mechanisms will prove inadequate; some of the deliberative processes will produce decisions that look bad in retrospect. None of this disqualifies the architecture. All of it is the normal cost of building anything new with imperfect humans on imperfect ground.

What disqualifies the existing arrangement is that it is failing in predictable ways, has been failing for a long time, and shows no capacity for self-correction. What recommends the architecture proposed here is not that it is perfect, but that it is designed for self-correction, capture-resistance, and continuous evolution by the people inside it. The architecture is alive in a way that the existing arrangement is not, because the existing arrangement is a fossil — beautifully preserved, in its way, but no longer attached to the conditions in which it once worked.

The grey man does not need a savior. He needs an architecture. This essay has tried to describe one. The next essays in this series will return to him — to his sovereignty, to the beauty that recovers his civic imagination, to the questions that remain when the institutions stop pretending to answer them for him. But first, this. The architecture. The structure. The thing that has to be built before anything else can stand on it.

## XI. The Move

If the previous essays asked the reader to recognize, this one asks him to build.

The building is small at first. A neighborhood assembly that actually meets, deliberates, and decides something. A cooperative whose governance is designed against capture from day one. A civic platform that functions as a demonstration of one piece of the architecture. A study group that learns the principles seriously and prepares to apply them locally. A network of such groups, coordinating across distances. The first phase of the architecture is not a national constitutional convention. It is a thousand small experiments, conducted seriously, by people who know what they are building toward.

The principles, repeated for clarity: coordination at scale without concentration. Capture-resistance by design. Legitimacy through process rather than charisma. Participation without exhaustion. Evolution without dissolution. Five layers of architecture: neighborhood, commune, region, federation, civilizational. Three roles: delegate, sortition citizen, steward. Six capabilities: identity, deliberation, decision, memory, communication, education. Eight anti-capture mechanisms, working together as a discipline rather than a list. Four phases of construction, measured in decades. One project, distributed across a country, building itself by hand.

This is the work. It does not march. It does not shout. It does not seize anything. It builds, in the small spaces available, the institutions that will, in time, render the captured ones unnecessary. The grey man has been waiting for permission to do this, all his life, because he was told the work was for someone else. It was never for someone else. It was always for him, and for the millions like him, and for the country they constitute when they stop waiting and start building.

The architecture is not the destination. The architecture is the trellis on which the actual republic — slow, patient, beautiful — will grow. The next essays will describe the republic itself: the sovereignty of the citizen who builds inside the architecture, the beauty that animates him, the daily practice of being a free person in a structure designed to keep him free.

But this had to come first. The structure. The blueprint. The promise that the demolition of the previous essays was not destruction but clearing, and that something is being built where the costumes used to stand.

The grey man does not need to seize power. He needs to build the architecture in which power, finally, is no longer the question.

That is the work. It begins, as all real work begins, on Monday morning, in the small place where you happen to be standing.

- *PLANCUIDADO* — care work, the substrate of contribution that the architecture recognizes.
- *PLANMESA* — food sovereignty.
