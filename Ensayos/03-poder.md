# The Fallacy of Power Itself
## An Essay on the Word That Has Owned Us

## I. The Word We Inherited Without Examining

There is a word so old, so central, so woven into how we speak of human affairs, that no one ever stops to ask whether it describes anything real. The word is *power*.

We say a president has power. We say a corporation has power. We say a movement gains power, that wealth confers power, that knowledge is power, that the powerful must be checked and the powerless must be lifted. We organize entire disciplines — political science, sociology, history, international relations — around the study of how power is acquired, distributed, exercised, and lost. The word is the master key. It opens every door in the house of politics.

And yet, if you stop and look carefully at what the word is supposed to denote, you will find nothing there. There is no substance called power. There is no organ in the body that produces it. There is no warehouse where it is stored, no metric that reliably measures it, no physical law that governs its conservation. The word names a phenomenon that is, on inspection, entirely relational and entirely contingent on the ongoing belief of the people involved in the relationship.

Power is not a thing one possesses. Power is a story other people tell about you, and obey.

This is not a clever rhetorical move. It is a literal description of how the phenomenon works. A president has power because soldiers obey his orders. The soldiers obey because they believe the orders are legitimate, or because they fear other soldiers who will punish disobedience, or because they have internalized a hierarchy taught to them since childhood. Remove the belief, the fear, or the hierarchy — at any point in the chain — and the power evaporates. It was never in the president. It was in the network of beliefs that made the president's words actionable.

We have, for thousands of years, taken a property of networks and lodged it in individuals. We have personified a relational pattern. And then, having created the illusion of a substance called power, we have spent the entirety of recorded history fighting over who gets to hold the substance, when the substance does not exist.

This is the deepest fallacy. We have organized civilization around a noun that is actually a verb, and around a possession that is actually a permission.

## II. The Three Lies Power Tells

Once you see the fiction, you start to notice the specific ways it deceives.

**The first lie: that power is a quantity.** We speak as if there were a fixed amount of it in any given system, so that for one person to have more, another must have less. This is the zero-sum frame that organizes nearly every political conflict on earth. It is also, demonstrably, false. The capacity for coordinated action in a society is not fixed. It can be expanded by trust, by communication, by shared infrastructure, by mutual recognition. It can be destroyed by their absence. Two strangers cooperating produce a kind of capacity that neither held in isolation, and neither lost by sharing. The zero-sum frame is not a description of reality. It is a useful fiction for those who profit from scarcity.

**The second lie: that power flows downward.** We imagine pyramids, hierarchies, chains of command. The king at the top, the people at the bottom, instructions descending and obedience ascending. But every political scientist who has actually studied how decisions are made in real institutions reports the same uncomfortable finding: power flows in every direction at once, constrained as much by those nominally below as by those nominally above. A general who issues an order his soldiers will not execute has issued nothing. A CEO who announces a strategy his employees will not implement has announced nothing. A president whose decrees are ignored is, in the only sense that matters, not a president. The pyramid is a flattering illusion held by people at the top, who mistake the deference of others for control over them.

**The third lie: that power is permanent.** The most seductive of the three. The powerful man imagines his power as a possession he can retain, defend, hand down. History laughs at him with grim regularity. The most powerful regimes in human history have collapsed, sometimes in days, when the underlying belief network that constituted them shifted. The Soviet Union held nuclear weapons, secret police, total control of media, and a quarter of the world's territory — and dissolved without a shot fired, because the people inside it stopped agreeing to act as if the power were real. The Berlin Wall fell because a confused border guard, unable to reach his superiors, decided to let people through. Power that took forty years to build evaporated in a single evening, because power had never been a substance. It had been a performance, and the performers had simply stopped performing.

Three lies, told together, constitute the dominant frame: power is fixed, hierarchical, and permanent. The frame is wrong on every point. It survives because it serves the people at the top of the imaginary pyramids, who have a strong interest in everyone else continuing to believe in their substance.

## III. What Actually Moves the World

If power is fiction, what is real? What actually causes coordinated human action?

Four things, in approximate order of underestimation:

**Contribution.** Most of what humans do for each other is not commanded, it is contributed. The food you ate today was grown, transported, sold, and prepared by people who were not following your orders. The language you think in was developed by millions of speakers across centuries, none of whom held a position over you. The infrastructure you depend on — roads, electricity, water, internet — was built by labor that does not appear in any organizational chart you belong to. The dominant mode of human coordination, by sheer volume, is voluntary contribution into systems that no one fully controls. Power-based coordination is a thin layer on top of a vast substrate of cooperation that proceeds without anyone in charge.

**Influence.** When humans do change each other's behavior, they do so primarily through the slow contagion of ideas, examples, and emotions, not through the issuance of commands. A teacher who shapes a generation has influenced more than most presidents. A mother who raises a child well has done more durable political work than most legislatures. A musician whose songs change how people feel about their lives has moved the world more than most armies. We acknowledge this in private and forget it in public. The history we write is the history of commanders. The history we live is the history of the influenced.

**Stewardship.** The longest-lasting institutions humans have built — religious orders, universities, certain crafts and guilds, indigenous governance traditions, the better cooperatives — are organized not around power but around stewardship. Someone holds the institution for a time, in trust, on behalf of others, with the understanding that they will hand it on. The framing is custodial, not proprietary. The stewardship frame produces patience, long horizons, and a kind of authority that does not corrode the holder, because the holder knows the authority is borrowed. Most of what we attribute to "good leadership" is actually good stewardship, mislabeled.

**Consent.** The bedrock. Every human coordination, ultimately, runs on consent — given, withheld, withdrawn, performed, or coerced. Even tyrannies depend on consent: the consent of the soldiers, the bureaucrats, the informers, the silent majority. Tyranny is not the absence of consent; it is the cultivation of consent through fear, ignorance, and isolation. When consent withdraws — really withdraws, at scale — the most powerful regime in history becomes a museum exhibit overnight. Consent is the only true sovereign. Power is the costume it wears when it has forgotten its own name.

These four — contribution, influence, stewardship, consent — describe, with reasonable accuracy, how human cooperation actually functions. None of them is well-described by the word *power*. All of them are obscured, daily, by the dominance of that word in our political vocabulary.

## IV. Why the Fiction Persists

A fiction this destructive does not survive for millennia by accident. It survives because it serves specific functions for specific people, and because it provides a particular psychological comfort to nearly everyone.

It serves the few because the frame of power justifies the unequal distribution of resources, attention, and deference. If power is real and you have it, then you deserve what comes with it. If power is fictional, then your wealth and authority are conditional, contested, and revocable — a much less comfortable position to occupy. The powerful have a strong interest in maintaining the substance theory of power, because the relational theory exposes them as borrowers rather than owners.

It serves the many because the frame of power outsources responsibility. If someone else has the power, then someone else is responsible for what happens. Your role is to obey, complain, vote, or rebel — but the locus of agency is somewhere else. The substance theory of power lets ordinary people perform passivity as virtue. *I am not responsible for the state of the country, because I am not powerful.* It is a comforting story. It is also a lie. The country is the cumulative behavior of its inhabitants, and no one's hands are clean.

It serves the academy because power is a tractable concept for analysis. You can map it, measure it, model it, write papers about its distribution. A relational, network-based, consent-driven understanding of human coordination is harder to study, harder to publish, harder to teach. The simpler fiction wins by methodological default.

It serves language itself, because we have built the entire vocabulary of politics around the noun. Take it away, and we are momentarily mute. We do not yet have the words for what comes next. This is not a small problem. Political revolutions are, first and last, revolutions in language. The vocabulary of contribution, influence, stewardship, and consent exists, but it has been pushed to the margins of how we talk about public life. Recovering it is the linguistic groundwork for the political shift.

So the fiction persists. The few defend it consciously. The many accept it unconsciously. The academy refines it. The language enforces it. And the world is run, badly, by a category error so deep that questioning it sounds, to most people, like questioning reality itself.

## V. The Argentine Mirror

Argentina is a country obsessed with power. Who has it. Who lost it. Who is coming for it. Who will hold it next. *La grieta* — the great rift — is, in essence, an argument over who deserves the substance, conducted by people who have never paused to ask whether the substance exists.

For decades, every faction has organized itself around the conquest of the state, because the state is where power is supposed to live. Capture the presidency, capture the congress, capture the courts, capture the central bank, and you will have power. Then, with power, you will fix the country.

It has not worked. It has never worked. Every faction that captured the state discovered, on arrival, that the state was less an instrument of power than a hostage of forces it could not control: international markets, entrenched bureaucracies, popular expectations, the cumulative weight of past decisions. The presidents who arrived at the Casa Rosada believing they had won power discovered, within months, that they had inherited constraints. The substance they had pursued was not there. There were only relationships, expectations, and dependencies — the same network of consent and contribution that runs every country, briefly mistaken for a throne.

The tragedy is not that any particular faction failed. The tragedy is that the country has spent forty years fighting over a fiction, while the actual substrate of national life — the schools, the hospitals, the roads, the small businesses, the neighborhoods, the communities of contribution and stewardship that constitute the country — has been neglected by everyone, because no one believed those things were where power lived.

They were where power always lived. The grieta is a fight over a costume, conducted while the body underneath has been wasting away.

This is not unique to Argentina. The same dynamic plays out, in different costumes, in every country where political life has been reduced to the conquest of imaginary substances. The countries that thrive are the countries where, for whatever local reason, the fiction of power has been weakest, and the real work of contribution and stewardship has been allowed to proceed without being constantly interrupted by people trying to capture a throne that does not exist.

## VI. The Reframe

Let us try, briefly, to speak without the word *power*, and see what becomes visible.

A president does not have power. A president has a temporary permission, granted by a network of beliefs and obediences, to issue instructions that some people will follow for some period of time. The permission can be withdrawn, narrowed, ignored, or overridden. The president's job, properly understood, is not to wield power but to steward a moment of permission on behalf of the people who granted it.

A wealthy person does not have power. A wealthy person has access to the labor of others, granted by a particular configuration of property law, monetary policy, and social convention. The configuration is contingent. It has been different in the past and will be different in the future. The wealth is real, in the sense that the access exists. But it is not a possession in the way the powerful imagine. It is a permission, also revocable.

A movement does not have power. A movement has the capacity to invite contribution, to attract attention, to shift the network of beliefs in which all other actors are operating. The most successful movements in history have been those that understood themselves as networks of contribution, not as armies seeking to conquer thrones. Civil rights, women's suffrage, the abolition of slavery — these were not victories of power against power. They were shifts in the underlying network of consent that made the old arrangements impossible to maintain.

A community does not need power. A community needs the practices, the rituals, the infrastructure, and the shared understanding that allow contribution, influence, stewardship, and consent to flow. Communities that have these things flourish, regardless of whether anyone holds office. Communities that lack these things suffer, regardless of how many offices they hold.

The reframe is not a denial of asymmetry. There are real asymmetries in the world — of resources, of attention, of voice, of historical injury. The reframe does not make those asymmetries disappear. It changes what they are, and therefore what can be done about them. An asymmetry of power is a substance to be redistributed. An asymmetry of permission and contribution is a relationship to be redesigned. The first frame produces revolutions that reproduce the structures they overthrew. The second frame produces architectures that dissolve the asymmetries by changing the conditions that sustained them.

This is the difference between fighting over a throne and dismantling the room the throne sits in.

The dismantling, however, is urgent in a way the reframe alone does not name, and that the rest of this essay must. Recognizing the fiction creates an opening. Openings get filled. The rooms our generation does not dismantle will be rebuilt, this very year, by actors who have substantial reasons to keep the fiction of power alive in a new costume — algorithmic, ambient, more total than the old kind, and harder to see because the new fiction is administered through infrastructures that look like services rather than thrones. The reframe makes the work possible. It does not do the work. And the window in which citizens can build different rooms — federated, transparent, owned by the people whose lives those rooms organize — is open today, by historical accident, and will not stay open by anyone's intention.

## VII. What Becomes Possible

Once power is recognized as a fiction, several things become possible that were not possible before.

Coordination at scale without hierarchy becomes thinkable. The dominant argument for hierarchy has always been that large groups need command structures to function. This is true if coordination is conceived as the imposition of will from above. It is false if coordination is conceived as the alignment of contribution from many. Modern technology, modern logistics, modern communication have made the second model viable at scales that the inventors of hierarchy could not have imagined. The internet runs without a president. Open-source software runs entire civilizations of code without a chief. Wikipedia is more accurate than the encyclopedias produced by hierarchies. The evidence is not theoretical. It is everywhere, and we refuse to see it because seeing it requires abandoning the fiction. The same logic — open, federated, citizen-built, governed by the people who use it — has barely begun to be applied to the rest of public life. It will be. The only question is by whom and on whose terms. Citizens who build the alternative architectures now, while the field is still open, set the conditions for the next century. Citizens who wait inherit the architectures that other people build during the wait.

Politics can become a craft of architecture rather than a contest of conquest. If there is no throne to be won, the political class as we know it has no function. What replaces it are the people who design coordination systems — the architects of institutions, protocols, deliberative processes, civic infrastructures. This is not a less interesting profession. It is a more interesting one. It rewards a different kind of mind: builders rather than performers, gardeners rather than warriors, stewards rather than sovereigns.

The citizen becomes sovereign in a new sense. Not sovereign as in ruler, but sovereign as in source. The citizen is the source from which all permission ultimately flows, the source of consent, the source of contribution, the source of the legitimacy that everyone above pretends to possess. Recognizing this is not a granting of new authority. It is the acknowledgment of authority that was always there, briefly obscured by the costume of power.

Justice becomes a question of architecture rather than of victory. Most of what we call injustice is the cumulative effect of architectures that channel contribution badly, distribute permission unfairly, and concentrate stewardship in the hands of the unaccountable. Fixing this is not a matter of winning a fight against the powerful. It is a matter of redesigning the channels. The fight is unwinnable in any final sense, because the powerful will always exist as long as the fiction of power exists. The redesign is possible, and durable.

Beauty becomes political. This is the part that sounds soft and is actually the most subversive. If power is a story, then the question of which stories we tell becomes the central political question. The aesthetic, the symbolic, the mythic — these are not decorations on top of real politics. They are the substrate from which the network of consent is woven. A people that has lost the capacity for beauty has lost the capacity for self-government, because they no longer have the symbolic resources to imagine themselves as anything other than what the dominant fiction tells them they are. The recovery of beauty — in language, in space, in ritual, in shared meaning — is the precondition for political recovery, not its reward.

These five shifts, taken together, constitute a different civilization. Not a utopia. A different operating system, with different bugs, different failure modes, different victories. The point is not that it would be perfect. The point is that the current operating system is built on a category error, and as long as the error stands, no amount of patching will fix the failures it produces.

## VIII. The Argument Against the Argument

I want to take seriously the strongest objection to everything I have just said, because the essay is not honest without it.

The objection is this: power may be a fiction, but it is a fiction that gets people killed. The man with the gun has a kind of reality that no philosophical reframe can dissolve. The state that decides who lives and who dies is not exercising a relational permission; it is exercising a brute capacity to inflict harm. To call this a fiction is to insult the people who have suffered under it.

The objection is correct, and the reframe must answer it directly.

What is real, in the man with the gun, is the gun. What is real, in the state that kills, is the violence. These are not fictions. They are the most concrete facts in human life. But the frame that organizes them — the legitimacy, the obedience, the chain of command, the willingness of soldiers to fire and bureaucrats to sign and citizens to comply — that is the network of consent, and that is what the word *power* has always actually named. The gun is not the power. The gun is an object. The power is the social arrangement that decides whose hand holds the gun, whose body it points at, and whether anyone protests when it fires.

This is not a denial of violence. It is a more precise diagnosis of how violence operates. The reframe does not make the man with the gun less dangerous. It makes the network around him visible, and therefore changeable. A regime of pure violence, with no underlying network of consent and obedience, has never existed and could not exist for a week. Every tyrant who has ever ruled has done so through the cooperation of soldiers, officials, informers, and citizens, the vast majority of whom were not personally violent. The reframe identifies the cooperators as the actual locus of what we call the tyrant's power. It also identifies the lever: when the cooperation withdraws, the tyranny ends.

The history of nonviolent resistance, properly understood, is the empirical proof of the reframe. Gandhi, King, Solidarity, the Velvet Revolution, the people of the Philippines in 1986, the Berlin Wall — these were not victories of one power against another. They were collapses of fictional substances, when enough of the cooperators withdrew their cooperation that the substance had nothing left to inhabit. The reframe is not a soft alternative to hard power politics. It is a more accurate description of how hard power politics has actually been defeated, every time it has actually been defeated.

This does not make the work easy. Withdrawing consent at scale, against an armed regime willing to kill, is the most dangerous and the most demanding form of political action humans have ever undertaken. Nothing in this essay should be read as suggesting otherwise. The reframe does not promise victory. It identifies the actual battlefield, which is not the throne room but the network of beliefs that sustains the throne room. That battlefield is where every real political transformation has been fought, whether or not the participants knew it.

## IX. The Beautiful Part

I want to end this essay where the previous two ended, with the grey man, because he is the figure in whom the reframe becomes most clearly true.

The grey man has been told, all his life, that he has no power. He looks at the powerful and sees their offices, their wealth, their access, their visibility, and he believes the comparison. He concludes that history is made by other people, that politics is for those who have the substance, that his role is to endure what is decided elsewhere.

He is wrong, and the wrongness is not a matter of consolation. It is a matter of fact.

Every regime he has lived under existed because he and people like him cooperated with it. Every wealthy person he has envied was wealthy because he and people like him bought what was sold. Every powerful person he has feared was powerful because he and people like him obeyed. The substance was never in the throne room. It was in the millions of small daily choices made by the people who never sat on the throne and never wanted to. He has been the source all along. He has been told the source was somewhere else, and he has believed it, because the belief was easier than the responsibility.

The recognition is not a celebration. It is a weight. To realize you have been the source of legitimacy for arrangements you did not approve of, for decades, is not a comfortable awakening. It is the kind of awakening that demands something of you. Not a vote. Not a protest. A change of life — in what you cooperate with, what you contribute to, what you steward, what you consent to. The political work begins where the awakening begins, in the small daily decisions whose cumulative pattern is the actual fabric of any country.

This is why the third essay in this series is the deepest cut. The first essay said: we do not need a president. The second essay said: we do not need them to choose one for us. The third essay says: power was never the question. The conversation has been, for millennia, an argument about who gets to hold a fiction. While we argued, the actual fabric of cooperation, contribution, stewardship, and consent has been under our hands the entire time. We were never weak. We were misdirected.

The end of the fallacy of power is not the beginning of powerlessness. It is the beginning of capacity. Because once you stop believing in the substance, you stop spending your life chasing it or resenting those who have it. You start asking different questions. What would I contribute, if I knew my contribution was the actual fabric? Whom would I influence, if I knew influence was the actual current? What would I steward, if I knew stewardship was the actual office? To what would I give my consent, and from what would I withdraw it, if I knew consent was the actual sovereign?

These are not philosophical questions. They are the most practical questions a citizen can ask. They have specific answers, in specific lives, leading to specific actions. They are the questions the powerful do not want you to ask, because the moment a population begins to answer them seriously, the costumes start to fall off the thrones, and the thrones begin to look like what they have always been: ordinary furniture, made by ordinary people, occupied by ordinary people who agreed to wear costumes for the duration of a temporary permission.

## X. The Move

The series began by criticizing a role. It then criticized a system. Now it criticizes the frame underneath both. The remaining essays will turn outward — to architecture, to the grey man himself, to beauty — but they all rest on this one. Without the recognition that power is a fiction, every alternative architecture eventually reproduces the pathology. With the recognition, alternative architectures become not only possible but inevitable, because the substrate they are built on — contribution, influence, stewardship, consent — is what was always actually running the world.

If you are reading this and wondering what to do on Monday, the answer is the same as the answer at the end of the previous essays, refined further. Stop performing the fiction. Stop talking about politics as if it were a contest of substances held by other people. Stop ceding your agency to figures who pretend to possess what no one possesses. Start asking, in your own life, what you are actually contributing to, who you are actually influencing, what you are actually stewarding, and what you are actually consenting to. Then make different answers, where different answers are possible. Not all at once. Not heroically. Daily.

A civilization in which a critical mass of citizens does this is a civilization that has dissolved the fallacy of power, not by defeating it, but by no longer feeding it. The thrones empty themselves, because the people who used to genuflect have stopped looking at them. The fiction becomes literature. The reality becomes politics again, for the first time in a very long time.

The grey man does not need to seize power. He needs to recognize that the fiction of power has been seizing him, all his life, by convincing him he was outside what he was actually inside. The end of that conviction is the beginning of everything else.

That is the revolution. It does not march. It does not shout. It does not storm any building. It simply, and quietly, and irreversibly, stops believing. And when enough people stop believing, the world that depended on their belief discovers that it was made of paper, and the wind has changed.
