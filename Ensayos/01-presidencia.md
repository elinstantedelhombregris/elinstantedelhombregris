# Why Presidents Are a Stupid Idea
## An Essay on the Architecture of Power

## I. The Inheritance We Forgot to Question

Every republic that calls itself modern still keeps a king in the attic. We renamed him. We gave him a term limit, a sash, a desk, an oath. We told ourselves the difference between a monarch and a president is the ballot box. But the architecture — one person, one will, one face on the currency of the imagination — is older than democracy and built for a world that no longer exists.

The presidency was designed in an era when information traveled at the speed of a horse, when coordinating a nation required a single point of decision because any other arrangement was logistically impossible. A president made sense the way a telegraph made sense: it was the best available technology for moving signal through a slow medium. We have since invented the internet, distributed databases, cryptographic consensus, real-time deliberation across continents. And yet the executive branch of nearly every republic still runs on horseback.

This is the first stupidity: we have preserved a coordination mechanism long after the constraint that justified it dissolved.

## II. The Information Problem

Russell Ackoff said it plainly: hierarchy is not a power structure, it is an information structure. We build pyramids when information is expensive and slow. We flatten them when information becomes cheap and fast.

A president is the apex of an information pyramid. Every meaningful decision in a country of forty-five million, or three hundred million, or a billion, is supposed to funnel — through advisors, through ministers, through filters — into the cognitive bandwidth of one human being who needs roughly eight hours of sleep, eats three meals a day, has a family, gets sick, gets tired, gets angry, falls in love, falls ill, ages.

The math does not work. It has never worked. What we call "presidential decisions" are almost always decisions made by anonymous staff and ratified by a signature. The president is, in operational reality, a bottleneck wearing a crown — and the crown is the problem, because it makes us believe a bottleneck is a leader.

This is the second stupidity: we have personified a function that no person can actually perform, and then we blame the person when the function fails.

What makes this worse, in our century, is that the pyramid did not flatten when the technology became cheap. It migrated. The information the presidency once needed and could not have — about the country, its people, its movements, its needs — now exists in unprecedented quantity, but most of it is held by infrastructures whose architecture was not built by us, whose interests are not aligned with ours, and whose owners we did not elect. The chair is still in the room. The country is being run from somewhere else.

## III. The Psychological Trap

Here is where it gets darker, and more interesting. The presidency is not just a bad org chart. It is a psychological technology that does specific things to the people who run for it, the people who win it, and the people who watch.

To the candidate, it offers the most intoxicating drug ever synthesized by political life: the belief that one person can save a country. Anyone willing to seek the office at the level required to win it has, almost by definition, already failed a basic sanity check. The role selects for messianism. It rewards people who can convince millions that their personal will is identical to the national will. This is not leadership. This is a particular kind of madness that we have institutionalized and named "ambition."

To the winner, it offers something worse: the slow, structural dissolution of contact with reality. The president is the last person in the country to hear bad news, the first person to be told they were right, the most surrounded human being in the nation, and somehow the loneliest. Every interaction is filtered. Every meal is prepared by someone whose job depends on the president's mood. Every disagreement is softened. The office is a sensory deprivation tank disguised as a throne.

To the public, it offers the most expensive psychological discount ever invented: the right to stop thinking about governance for four years. If there is a president, then someone is in charge. If someone is in charge, then problems are their fault, not ours. The presidency lets a population outsource its civic responsibility to a single face and then blame that face when reality fails to obey. The "messianic delegation" is not a bug of the system. It is its core service.

This is the third stupidity: the office damages the person who holds it, and infantilizes the people who created it.

## IV. The Argentine Mirror (and Every Other Mirror)

Argentina has had presidents for a long time. Tyrants and technocrats, the charismatic and the quiet, the corrupt and the honest. Radical, conservative, Peronist, anti-Peronist, military, civilian, populist, liberal, libertarian. The faces have changed. The cycles have not. Every six or eight years the country falls in love with a new face. Every six or eight years it falls out of love and blames that face for structural conditions no face could fix. Every six or eight years a new candidate appears and says *this time it will be different, because of me*, and a population that has learned nothing from the previous twelve answers, *yes, because of you*.

The cycles have outlasted every face that wore them, and they will outlast the next twelve, because the country has been running an experiment whose results are now decades long: changing the person at the top while leaving the architecture of the country in hands that do not answer to its citizens. Not only the presidency. Also the systems that record who we are, that move our money, that hold our memory, that mediate our conversations, that decide what reaches our children. We have argued for more than four decades about which face should occupy the chair, while the rooms behind the chair — where our identity is administered, our payments processed, our deliberations surfaced or buried, our archives held — were quietly turned over to actors who never had to win an election here, and never will. A country that fights about its presidents while its nervous system runs on infrastructures that belong to other people is a country that has misidentified its sovereignty problem.

This is not a story about Argentina. It is a story about presidencies, and about everything they distract from. The names are different in Brazil, Mexico, France, the Philippines, the United States. The structure is the same. We are not looking at twelve failures of leadership but at one structural failure repeating itself with different costumes — and a system that produces the same outcome regardless of who runs it does not need better candidates. It needs to be redesigned. The redesign is not the next ballot. It is the building.

## V. What "Stupid" Actually Means

I want to be careful here. "Stupid" is not an insult, it is a technical term. A design is stupid when it produces outcomes opposite to its stated purpose with high reliability. The presidency is stupid in this exact sense:

It claims to concentrate accountability and instead diffuses it — because everything that goes wrong is blamed on "the previous government" or "the opposition" or "the deep state."

It claims to enable decisive action and instead produces gridlock — because a single person opposing a legislature creates more friction than collaborative bodies coordinating among themselves.

It claims to represent the will of the people and instead represents the will of whichever fifty-one percent showed up on a Tuesday — and then governs the other forty-nine as if they consented.

It claims to be a check on tyranny and is in fact the most efficient infrastructure for tyranny ever designed, because anyone who captures it captures everything attached to it.

A design that does the opposite of what it promises, reliably, across centuries and continents, is not unfortunate. It is stupid. The word fits.

A design this stupid will not survive forever — but the question is not whether it will be replaced. It is by what, and on whose terms. The presidency is being made obsolete in our lifetime. The architecture that replaces it is being chosen now, by the people who get there first.

## VI. What Could Replace It

Most critiques get cowardly here. They diagnose and refuse to prescribe, because prescribing is harder than diagnosing. So let me be specific, even at the risk of being wrong.

The first instinct, when you pull down a single throne, is to put up several smaller ones. Replace the president with a council. A seven-person rotating cabinet. A parliamentary cabinet that meets in the morning and signs things in the afternoon. Switzerland has run such a council for over a century without producing a single household-name president — a partial historical example that the executive function does not require a face. Take the example and walk past it. Seven thrones is still thrones. The problem was never how many people sat in the chair. The problem was the chair.

The answer is to remove the chair entirely and replace it with the only thing that can do its work in a country of forty-five million: **infrastructure that allows the citizenry to perform the executive function themselves, continuously, at the scale where the consequences land.**

Say it cleanly. The alternative to a president is not a smaller group of presidents. It is a national civic information stack — a working architecture, owned and governed by the citizens it serves, designed to do the things presidents pretend to do and cannot. To surface the country's priorities from the people who live them. To deliberate at every layer where deliberation belongs. To decide at the scale where the decision lands. To convert the collective intelligence of a nation into coordinated action without funneling it through the cognitive bottleneck of a single human being.

In the civic register: citizens contribute what they know — about their street, their school, their water, their work. The system aggregates without flattening. Deliberation surfaces where it would otherwise be drowned by a feed engineered for outrage. The decisions that touch one neighborhood are made by that neighborhood; the decisions that touch a region, by the region; the decisions that touch a country, by the country, deliberating slowly and well, with everyone who has reason to weigh in able to weigh in. Action follows. Not by the signature of one tired person at the top. By the coordinated movement of millions whose judgment was actually consulted.

The technical pieces exist. Identity that the citizen carries like a key, not a permission a platform grants. Deliberation infrastructure that is not engineered to maximize her engagement but to hold her arguments. Decision tooling — sortition, liquid delegation, participatory budgeting — that runs at neighborhood scale, citizen-runnable, not platform-mediated. Civic memory in archives that no future government and no future quarterly board can revoke. Communication layers that do not reward rage. Education in the open. None of this is exotic. All of it has been built, in pieces, in different countries, by people who knew what they were doing. What is missing is the will to build it as our own — sovereign, accountable, capture-resistant by protocol design and not by promise — and the knowledge that if we do not build it, someone else will.

That last part is the part most critiques will not say out loud. Let me say it.

**The greatest act of freedom available to us in this century is not to cross the mountain with an army. It is to build the tools that allow us to organize ourselves, govern ourselves, and live meaningful lives at the scale of a country — before someone else builds those tools for us, on terms that are not ours.**

Interests are already racing to install their civic infrastructure into the vacuum left by collapsing democracies. Corporate, governmental, foreign, ideological — different actors with different motives, and one shared assumption: that the architecture which mediates the next century of public life will be theirs. They are not waiting for our permission. They are not asking what we would build. They are building.

If we do not build, the cost is not abstract. It is a country whose civic memory is held in archives a foreign company can close on a Tuesday. A country whose citizens authenticate themselves with a permission that a platform can revoke without explanation. A country whose deliberations run on systems engineered by people who will never know our names, tuned for outcomes that maximize their revenue and not our lives. A country whose decisions are augmented by systems trained on values we did not choose and on data we did not consent to give. Each of these is happening already, in small ways, in every country including ours. None of them is irreversible if we build before the building is done for us. All of them harden into the architecture of the next century if we wait.

The window is open today. It will not be open in twenty years. The tools that mediate civic life by 2046 are being designed now, by people whose interests are not ours, and the longer we delay the harder the displacement gets. This is not panic. It is sobriety. It is the sober recognition by people who have understood the stakes that the building has to be done now, by us, or it will be done later, against us.

The Swiss Council was an example. The infrastructure is the answer.

## VII. The Beautiful Part

Here is what I actually believe, and why I am writing this instead of just criticizing.

A country without a president is not a country without leadership. It is a country where leadership becomes a property of the network rather than a property of an individual. Where decisions are traceable to the people who actually made them, not to a face on a poster. Where citizens stop waiting every four years to be saved and start, slowly, to govern themselves in the small daily ways that actually constitute a republic — and to *build* the infrastructure that lets that self-government scale beyond the daily.

The grey man — the ordinary citizen, the one who cooks, drives, teaches, builds, repairs — does not need a president. He needs roads, courts, schools, water, electricity, and the dignity of being treated as an adult by his institutions. He needs all of that. And he needs the tools that let him and the millions like him run a country together — the identity that is his, the deliberation that holds his argument, the memory that cannot be revoked, the decisions taken at the scale where the consequences fall on him. He is not the recipient of these tools. He is, with the millions like him, the one who builds them.

This does not mean every grey man writes the protocol. The work is too large for any of us alone, and small enough that none of us is unnecessary. Some of us deliberate. Some adjudicate. Some teach. Some carry the wood. Some write the code. The citizenry governs the building; accountable specialists execute under that governance. The architecture lives only because every layer answers to the citizens it serves, and never the other way around. There is no priesthood. There are no founders to be deferred to. There is the work, distributed across the ones who chose to do it.

The deepest stupidity of the presidency is not even structural. It is spiritual. It teaches us, every electoral cycle, that we are children waiting for a parent. That salvation comes from above. That history is made by great men. None of this is true. History is made by millions of small decisions, and the great men are mostly along for the ride, taking credit for the wave they happened to surf. A republic that grows up is a republic that stops needing a face. It learns that power is a verb, not a noun. It distributes what was concentrated, makes transparent what was hidden, returns to the people what was always theirs in principle and almost never theirs in practice.

But it grows up only by building. The infrastructure of an adult republic does not appear when the citizens are ready for it; the citizens become ready for it by building it. This is the labor that no candidate can do for us, that no election can substitute for, that no theory can deliver. Our generation will not see all of it finished. The first generation builds. The next inherits. That is fair. That is, in fact, exactly what it means to leave a country to your children.

We do not need to abolish the presidency tomorrow. We need to stop pretending it is the natural order of things, and we need to start building the architecture that will, slowly and quietly, make it obsolete. We need to look at the chair the way a future archaeologist will look at it: as a curious transitional object between monarchy and something we had to have the courage to build ourselves.

## VIII. The Move

If you are reading this and thinking *yes, but what do I do on Monday* — that is the right question. Here is the honest answer.

You build the alternatives at the scale you can reach. A neighborhood assembly that actually decides something. A cooperative that runs without a charismatic founder. A civic platform whose architecture cannot be captured by any single person, including you. A community that practices distributed decision-making in small things so that, when the time comes, it can practice it in big ones. Each of these is a node — not a metaphor for the larger thing, but a working piece of the larger thing. The way a single neighborhood assembly that genuinely decides something is a working piece of a self-governing country and not a rehearsal for one.

Some of this is invention. Most of it is forking — taking what citizens have already built somewhere else, somewhere it works, and adapting it for here. Heroic from-scratch construction is not the bar. Forking what works, federating it, governing it together — that is most of the labor. The work is large. It is also already half-done in fifty places, by people who do not know each other yet, and whose tools will eventually have to talk.

Republics do not change because someone wins an election. They change because, underneath the elections, a different way of organizing has quietly become possible. The presidency will not be abolished by a candidate. It will be made obsolete by the parallel construction of something better — by the patient, distributed, sometimes invisible work of building the infrastructure of an adult republic before the people who would build it for us finish their own version.

That is not a defeat. That is the work. And it is, for what it is worth, the most beautiful work there is — because it returns to ordinary people the agency that was taken from them by the polite fiction that someone else was in charge.

The grey man does not need a president. He needs only to remember that he was always the one holding the country up — and that the country he is holding up is not finished yet.
