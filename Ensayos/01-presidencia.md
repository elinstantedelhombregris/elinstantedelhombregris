# Why Presidents Are a Stupid Idea
## An Essay on the Architecture of Power

## I. The Inheritance We Forgot to Question

Every republic that calls itself modern still keeps a king in the attic. We renamed him. We gave him a term limit, a sash, a desk, an oath. We told ourselves the difference between a monarch and a president is the ballot box. But the architecture — one person, one will, one face on the currency of the imagination — is older than democracy and built for a world that no longer exists.

The presidency was designed in an era when information traveled at the speed of a horse, when coordinating a nation required a single point of decision because any other arrangement was logistically impossible. A president made sense the way a telegraph made sense: it was the best available technology for moving signal through a slow medium. We have since invented the internet, distributed databases, cryptographic consensus, real-time deliberation across continents. And yet the executive branch of nearly every republic still runs on horseback.

This is the first stupidity: we have preserved a coordination mechanism long after the constraint that justified it dissolved.

## II. The Information Problem

Russell Ackoff said it plainly: hierarchy is not a power structure, it is an information structure. We build pyramids when information is expensive and slow. We flatten them when information becomes cheap and fast.

A president is the apex of an information pyramid. Every meaningful decision in a country of forty-five million, or three hundred million, or a billion, is supposed to funnel — through advisors, through ministers, through filters — into the cognitive bandwidth of one human being who needs roughly eight hours of sleep, eats three meals a day, has a family, gets sick, gets tired, gets angry, falls in love, falls ill, ages.

The math does not work. It has never worked. What we call "presidential decisions" are almost always decisions made by anonymous staff and ratified by a signature. The president is, in operational reality, a bottleneck wearing a crown — and the crown is the problem, because it makes us believe a bottleneck is a leader.

This is the second stupidity: we have personified a function that no person can actually perform, and then we blame the person when the function fails.

## III. The Psychological Trap

Here is where it gets darker, and more interesting. The presidency is not just a bad org chart. It is a psychological technology that does specific things to the people who run for it, the people who win it, and the people who watch.

To the candidate, it offers the most intoxicating drug ever synthesized by political life: the belief that one person can save a country. Anyone willing to seek the office at the level required to win it has, almost by definition, already failed a basic sanity check. The role selects for messianism. It rewards people who can convince millions that their personal will is identical to the national will. This is not leadership. This is a particular kind of madness that we have institutionalized and named "ambition."

To the winner, it offers something worse: the slow, structural dissolution of contact with reality. The president is the last person in the country to hear bad news, the first person to be told they were right, the most surrounded human being in the nation, and somehow the loneliest. Every interaction is filtered. Every meal is prepared by someone whose job depends on the president's mood. Every disagreement is softened. The office is a sensory deprivation tank disguised as a throne.

To the public, it offers the most expensive psychological discount ever invented: the right to stop thinking about governance for four years. If there is a president, then someone is in charge. If someone is in charge, then problems are their fault, not ours. The presidency lets a population outsource its civic responsibility to a single face and then blame that face when reality fails to obey. The "messianic delegation" is not a bug of the system. It is its core service.

This is the third stupidity: the office damages the person who holds it, and infantilizes the people who created it.

## IV. The Argentine Mirror (and Every Other Mirror)

Argentina has had presidents for a long time. Some were tyrants, some were technocrats, some were charismatic, some were quiet, some were corrupt, some were honest. The country has been radical, conservative, Peronist, anti-Peronist, military, civilian, populist, liberal, libertarian. The faces have changed. The cycles have not.

Every six or eight years the country falls in love with a new face. Every six or eight years it falls out of love and blames that face for structural conditions no face could fix. Every six or eight years a new candidate appears and says: this time it will be different, because of me. And a population that has learned nothing from the previous twelve says: yes, because of you.

This is not a story about Argentina. It is a story about presidencies. The names are different in Brazil, Mexico, France, the Philippines, the United States. The structure is the same. We are not looking at twelve failures of leadership but at one structural failure repeating itself with different costumes — and a system that produces the same outcome regardless of who runs it does not need better candidates. It needs to be redesigned.

## V. What "Stupid" Actually Means

I want to be careful here. "Stupid" is not an insult, it is a technical term. A design is stupid when it produces outcomes opposite to its stated purpose with high reliability. The presidency is stupid in this exact sense:

It claims to concentrate accountability and instead diffuses it — because everything that goes wrong is blamed on "the previous government" or "the opposition" or "the deep state."

It claims to enable decisive action and instead produces gridlock — because a single person opposing a legislature creates more friction than collaborative bodies coordinating among themselves.

It claims to represent the will of the people and instead represents the will of whichever fifty-one percent showed up on a Tuesday — and then governs the other forty-nine as if they consented.

It claims to be a check on tyranny and is in fact the most efficient infrastructure for tyranny ever designed, because anyone who captures it captures everything attached to it.

A design that does the opposite of what it promises, reliably, across centuries and continents, is not unfortunate. It is stupid. The word fits.

## VI. What Could Replace It

This is the part where most critiques get cowardly. They diagnose and refuse to prescribe, because prescribing is harder than diagnosing. So let me be specific, even at the risk of being wrong.

**Distribute the executive function.** Modern republics already have councils, cabinets, and collegial bodies. Make them real. Switzerland has run a seven-person rotating Federal Council for over a century without producing a single household-name president, and it is by most measures one of the best-governed countries on earth. The fact that you cannot name the current Swiss president is not a bug. It is the entire point.

**Separate the symbolic from the operational.** Humans need ritual, ceremony, a face for the nation. Fine. Give them one. But do not give that face the nuclear codes. Parliamentary republics do this routinely: a ceremonial president, an operational prime minister accountable to the legislature, and a cabinet that functions as a real collective body rather than a chorus of yes-men.

**Make decisions match their scale.** Most things a president decides should not be decided by a president. They should be decided by the people closest to the consequences — municipalities, regions, professional bodies, affected communities — using deliberative and digital tools that did not exist when the office was invented. The principle is old: subsidiarity. The technology to implement it is new.

**Build anti-capture mechanisms into the architecture itself.** Hard term limits. Radical transparency. Fork rights — the ability of communities to exit and reconstitute. Sunset clauses on every authority. A presidency can be captured by a person, a party, a donor class, or a foreign power. A well-designed distributed executive cannot, because there is no single point to capture.

None of this is utopian. Pieces of it exist already, in different countries, working. The stupidity is not that we lack alternatives. The stupidity is that we keep choosing the worst option because it is the most familiar.

## VII. The Beautiful Part

Here is what I actually believe, and why I am writing this instead of just criticizing.

A country without a president is not a country without leadership. It is a country where leadership becomes a property of the network rather than a property of an individual. Where decisions are traceable to the people who actually made them, not to a face on a poster. Where citizens stop waiting every four years to be saved and start, slowly, to govern themselves in the small daily ways that actually constitute a republic.

The grey man — the ordinary citizen, the one who cooks, drives, teaches, builds, repairs — does not need a president. He needs roads, courts, schools, water, electricity, and the dignity of being treated as an adult by his institutions. None of those things require a king. All of them require the boring, distributed, unglamorous work of building systems that function regardless of who is in charge.

The deepest stupidity of the presidency is not even structural. It is spiritual. It teaches us, every electoral cycle, that we are children waiting for a parent. That salvation comes from above. That history is made by great men. None of this is true. History is made by millions of small decisions, and the great men are mostly along for the ride, taking credit for the wave they happened to surf.

A republic that grows up is a republic that stops needing a face. It learns that power is a verb, not a noun. It distributes what was concentrated, makes transparent what was hidden, returns to the people what was always theirs in principle and almost never theirs in practice.

We do not need to abolish the presidency tomorrow. We need to stop pretending it is the natural order of things. We need to look at it the way a future archaeologist will look at it: as a curious transitional object between monarchy and something we have not yet had the courage to build.

## VIII. The Move

If you are reading this and thinking yes, but what do I do on Monday — that is the right question. Here is the honest answer.

You build the alternatives at the scale you can reach. A neighborhood assembly that actually decides something. A cooperative that runs without a charismatic founder. A civic platform whose architecture cannot be captured by any single person, including you. A community that practices distributed decision-making in small things so that, when the time comes, it can practice it in big ones.

Republics do not change because someone wins an election. They change because, underneath the elections, a different way of organizing has quietly become possible. The presidency will not be abolished by a candidate. It will be made obsolete by the parallel construction of something better.

That is not a defeat. That is the work. And it is, for what it is worth, the most beautiful work there is — because it returns to ordinary people the agency that was taken from them by the polite fiction that someone else was in charge.

The grey man does not need a president. He needs only to remember that he was always the one holding the country up.
