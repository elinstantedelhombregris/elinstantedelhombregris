# Carta al Nieto
## A Letter to the Grandchild Not Yet Born

By the time you read this, I will probably be gone. Not certainly — I have been wrong about death often enough, the way old men are — but probably. The letter sits in a drawer in your mother's kitchen, in a house she does not yet have, in a country that does not yet exist as the country I am addressing you from. So everything I say here is being said into a fog. That is fine. Most of what matters is said into a fog.

You are not yet born. Your mother is still a child today, or perhaps a young woman, or perhaps not even decided to be a mother. The letter is going to wait — in this drawer, on a shelf, in a box that follows the family from one move to another — until the moment when somebody, perhaps you, perhaps your mother thinking of you, opens it. So I will write as if you are reading, because by the time you are reading, that will be true.

I want to tell you what I know, and what I learned, and what your great-grandfather did not have the words for, and what your father and mother are still finding the words for, and what you, almost certainly, will not need words for at all because you will have been born into the country they are in the middle of building.

This is the letter I wish someone had written me when I was on the 60 to Constitución at six in the morning, thinking about my daughter's school shoes — your aunt, by then, but in my memory still the small body who held my hand and asked me if everything would be alright.

Let me set down what I want you to know.

* * *

There were six essays in a box of papers I left for you. They were written by a man who could see something he could not yet name. He wrote them when he was forty, in 2026 — your great-grandparents' time, the year your country and your continent were still being told they were broken in ways they were not actually broken.

You may read those essays. Or you may not. If you do, you will find them argumentative and a little angry, in the way that men are angry when they have just figured something out and want everyone else to see it. They are not wrong. I have re-read them many times. But they are not the letter, either. They were the scaffolding. *You are reading the building.*

What the essays say, in the long arguments and the careful sections, comes down to a few things. We did not need a president. We did not need them to choose one for us. The thing we were calling power was a story we had been telling each other for too long. The architecture you live in was not natural; it was built, slowly, by people not very different from you, in their kitchens, on their blocks, in the small spaces where they happened to be standing. The you that you call *myself* was the source the whole time. And beauty was the fuel — without it the whole project would have run out of energy by the time you were born.

That is what the essays say. They say it well, and the man who wrote them was proud of them. But they say it from outside the country we were trying to make. I am writing to you from inside the country we made. The view is different.

Let me show you what I see.

* * *

The country looks more like the country than it did. That is the strangest thing to say, but the truest. It does not look futuristic. There are no chrome buildings. The bus you take to school is, possibly, a descendant of the 60 I used to take to Constitución, and the seats are no nicer. You will eat asado on Sundays the way I did, and your great-grandparents did, and so on backward beyond memory. Mate will pass from hand to hand, and you will burn yourself on the metal bombilla the way every child has, and you will be told, as I was told, that this is how you learn.

What is different is harder to point to.

The neighborhood I live in meets every fifteen days, in a hall that used to be a *sociedad de fomento* half-broken in the 1990s and is now polished and full of light. We are forty-two households who have known each other long enough that we know who can be trusted to count the bills, and who is going through something hard, and who needs to be reminded to take their medication. Twelve of us, drawn by lottery, sit at a long table in front of the others. Six are *delegados*; six are *sorteados*. They rotate every year. I have been every kind of person on that table at one time or another — when I was younger and angrier, a delegado; when I was older and quieter, a sorteado; once, briefly, a steward, looking after the small library that the asamblea uses for its papers. The library has my fingerprints on every other book.

This is not heroic. It is administrative. Most of the work of governing yourselves is administrative — water, paving, the question of whether the corner where the children play needs a different kind of light. But the administration is yours, and you know the people who decide it, and they know you, and the things that get decided actually happen. That alone, in the country I was born into, would have seemed like fiction. To you it will seem like Tuesday.

You will not have a president. The country has not had one since you were a glimmer in your parents' eyes, and by the time you can read this, the institution will be a footnote in your history class. There is a council of seven, in the federation building in Buenos Aires, that does what a president used to be imagined to do, and rotates often enough that nobody knows the names of the seven without checking. The newspapers do not put their faces on covers, because the newspapers have learned that faces on covers do not sell papers in a country where citizens have stopped looking up. The political gossip you will hear, in the kiosko or in the verdulería, will be about the asamblea on the corner, or the commune that just opened a new clinic, or the steward of the river basin who is about to retire. That is the gossip a free country produces. It is local because the politics is local.

*La grieta* is not gone. People still disagree about everything that has ever divided this country. But the disagreement no longer eats us, because it has somewhere to go that is not the conquest of a throne. When two halves of a neighborhood disagree about a school, they have an asamblea, and the asamblea decides, and the half that lost has the right to fork — to organize a different school, on different terms, if the disagreement is deep enough. They almost never do. The fork right was designed to be available so that it would rarely have to be used. But it is there. And because it is there, the half that lost knows it lost honestly, and the half that won knows it has to govern as if the loser could leave at any moment.

This will sound impossibly dull to you. Asambleas, councils, fork rights, sorteados. I am writing it down because *the dullness is the victory*. I lived through cycles that made this dullness sound like utopia. You will live with the dullness as the texture of an ordinary day, and you will probably be bored by it, and that is exactly what your great-grandparents prayed you would be.

* * *

I want to tell you what the work felt like, because by the time you read this it will probably look inevitable, and I do not want you to lose the dignity of knowing that it was not.

When I was the age you will be when you first read this letter — twelve, fifteen, twenty — there was no architecture. There was a country in pieces, and a small group of people who had stopped waiting for someone to fix it. We met in a kitchen, the first time, in 2026. Five of us. Three of us are still alive. One is your grandmother. One is the woman who later married into your father's family. The fifth, who I loved like a brother, did not live to see the country we are in now. He should have.

We did not know what we were doing. We met because we had read a manifesto, and the manifesto had named what was wrong in a way that we had been feeling but had not had the vocabulary for. We met because the country was on fire, in its slow Argentine way, and the people who were supposed to put out fires were the ones holding the matches. We met because we did not want to die, twenty years later, having known what to do and not done it.

So we started. Not with a national plan. With an asamblea on our block — twelve households at first, six of which thought we were crazy. With a *chequeo* — a check, a kind of small audit — of the budget the city was supposed to be spending on our calle. With a question to the verdulero about why the cooperative he used to belong to had collapsed, and what would have been needed to keep it alive. The questions accumulated. Other people, a few at a time, became part of the kitchen. Then there were two kitchens. Then five. Then a network. The network had a name we gave ourselves, half-joking and half-serious: ¡BASTA!. *Because that is what an exhausted population finally says when it stops waiting to be saved.*

The work was beautiful, and it was tiring, and most of the time it was unrecognizable as political work. We organized a tool library. We learned to do the budget of our own neighborhood. We taught the children of our block how to plant tomatoes on a balcony. We held a meeting once a month where anyone could come and propose what the block should do next. People came. People disagreed. People sometimes screamed at each other. Then they ate together, because we always ate together, because the asado is older than the architecture, and we knew without saying it that *the asado was the architecture*.

You should know that almost nothing we did made the news. The news was, in those years, fixated on the visible classes — the candidates, the scandals, the cycles. Our work happened underneath. It was not glamorous. It did not produce charismatic leaders. It produced, slowly, a country.

Then the federation cracked, in the way captured systems crack — not from being attacked, but from no longer corresponding to the way people had begun to live. The grandchildren of the dictatorship, the children of forty years of crisis, looked at the federation and at the network of asambleas and concluded that they preferred the asambleas. The transition took six years. It was peaceful. It was not glamorous. It was, mostly, paperwork. Forty thousand asambleas signed the new federal compact in the spring of 2042, in a wave that had been building for fifteen years. By the time it happened, almost no one was surprised.

That is how the country you live in came to exist. It was not heroic. It was not a battle. It was a thousand small kitchens that became a network and a network that became a federation. The person who did the most heroic-sounding thing in those decades was a girl your age who climbed onto a roof in Lanús to take down the banner of a candidate nobody believed in anymore. The roof was higher than her grandmother had told her was safe. The banner came down. Nobody cared about the candidate after that. The story made the local news for a day.

I am telling you all this because I want you to understand that what you have was made by people not very different from you. They had headaches. They had bad arguments with their mothers. They had long jobs that paid badly. They did not believe themselves to be making history. They were just refusing, daily, to participate in the country that was being run for them, and refusing, daily, to believe that the work was for someone else.

You are reading this letter because they refused. *That is the whole story.*

* * *

There is one thing about those years I want you to know, because by the time you read this it will sound like history and at the time it was anything but. The country you live in came close to not being possible. The archives that hold what we know about ourselves were nearly held in places we did not own, by people who had every right to close them. The credentials by which a citizen was a citizen were nearly permissions a foreign company could revoke without explanation. The deliberations of an entire continent were running on systems engineered to keep us angry, and the systems that were beginning to think for us were already being trained, patiently, on values that were not ours and on data we had not consented to give.

We built the cathedrals of the civic age in time. It was close. *The being-in-time was the work* — the work of every kitchen that became a network and every network that became a federation, while interests with substantial reasons to keep us inert were racing to install their own architecture in the empty space our democracies were leaving. We did not stop them by force. We built before they finished. The next generation will face its own version of the same race, with different actors and the same logic. The work of being in time will be yours.

That is the part of the story the essays did not yet know. By the time I am writing this, we know it. I am writing it down for you so that you will know it too, and so that you will know, when your own version of the race begins, that the people who built the country you live in won theirs not by being heroic but by being early.

* * *

I want to leave you, from me, six small things. They are the things I learned that the essays did not say, because the essays were written before I had lived enough to know them.

**The first is mate.** Not mate as a drink — you will know that — but mate as a discipline. The way the gourd passes. The way you wait. The way you let the hot water do what it does. Mate teaches you what democracy actually feels like. There is one drinker at a time, and everyone in the circle waits, and the waiting is not impatient because the gourd is going to come around. A circle of people drinking mate is the smallest functional asamblea in human history. Pay attention to it. Most of what you need to know about how to be with other people is in the gourd.

**The second is the asado.** Not the meal, the labor. The making of the fire. The patience for the coals. The conversation around the parrilla. The unhurried friendship between the carne and the time. An asado teaches you that the most important things have to be done slowly. The country we used to have was a country that wanted everything fast. The country you live in is a country that has remembered the asado.

**The third is your hand.** Learn one thing your hand can do that no app can do. It does not matter what it is. Plant a tomato. Bake bread. Sew a button. Tune a guitar. Build a chair. The hand that knows one thing knows what your body is for. The hand that knows nothing forgets that you are a being who makes. *A people who have remembered their hands cannot be told they are powerless.* I learned this late, after a lifetime of not having to repair things, and I wish someone had told me sooner.

**The fourth is the no.** You will be asked to say yes to many things in your life that you should not say yes to. The no is your sovereignty. It does not have to be heroic. Most of the no's that matter are quiet. The no to the corruption you were asked to tolerate. The no to the meeting that has no purpose. The no to the comfortable lie. The no to the work that pays well but harms the world. Each small no is a small recovery. The cumulative no's, across a population, are the country defending itself.

**The fifth is the table.** Eat with the people you love, regularly, without screens. Celebrate the small holidays the way your great-grandparents did. Teach your own children the songs and the stories and the recipes that constitute your particular thread in the larger tapestry. This is not nostalgia. It is infrastructure. The hearth is where citizens are made. A country of strong tables is a country that cannot be captured for long.

**The sixth is the appointment with the sublime.** Once a week, if you can — once a month, if you must — go somewhere where the night sky is still visible, or to a building made by people who knew that humans need to feel small in the face of something greater, or to the river, or to the mountain. Stay long enough that you remember you are a being whose life is shorter than the things around you. The captured economy will keep you away from these encounters. Keep them anyway. The republic depends on citizens who have known the sublime, and the knowing has to be cultivated, deliberately, against the relentless pressure of the trivial.

These six are not enough. There are others. But these are the ones I am most sure of, and the ones I have most regularly seen citizens of your country lose and regret losing. If you keep them, you will be most of what your great-grandparents prayed you would be.

* * *

There are things I cannot give you, and I want to name them, because not naming them would be a kind of theft.

**I cannot give you a country without grief.** Your great-grandfather's bones still ache in this country. The disappeared are still missing. The cycles of crisis we lived through left scars that will outlive me, and probably you. The architecture we built is not a healing. It is a way of living with what cannot be healed. You will encounter, in your life, sorrows that the country has carried for generations. You will be tempted to think that my generation, having built the architecture, should also have ended the grief. We could not. Grief is not a structural problem. It is the price of having been here long enough to love.

**I cannot give you a country without ugliness.** There are still ugly streets, ugly meals, ugly conversations. The architecture made beauty more available, but not automatic. Each generation has to choose to make beautiful things, and the choosing has to be daily. If you stop choosing, the ugliness returns, because the captured aesthetic is patient and never tires. I have seen, in my lifetime, the recovery of beauty in some places and its retreat in others. *The recovery is not finished. The work is not finished. It will not be finished by you either.*

**I cannot give you a country that has resolved the question of violence.** We made a more peaceful country by giving people somewhere to put their conflicts that was not the conquest of a state. But there is still violence. There are still men who hurt women. There are still families that hurt their own. There are still people on the edges of every neighborhood whose lives are smaller than they should be, and whose smallness sometimes hardens into harm. The architecture provides for this — sortition tribunals, restorative councils, accountable use of force at the regional layer — but the architecture does not abolish it. Be a person who can stand near violence and not pretend it is not there. The architecture needs citizens of that kind.

**I cannot give you certainty about the future.** The architecture you live in has worked for forty years. Forty years is not long. There were architectures that worked for two centuries and then crumbled. There were architectures that worked for two thousand. None of them lasted forever. The architecture you live in could be captured, in your lifetime, by something we did not anticipate. The fork rights are there. The anti-capture mechanisms are there. The vigilance is yours. If, when you are sixty, you find yourself looking at a country that no longer recognizes itself, the work that we did was not wasted, but the work that you must do has already begun.

**I cannot give you the assurance that what you do will matter.** I think it will. I lived long enough to see that what I did mattered, in ways I did not expect. But there is no guarantee. Some of the work we did did not survive. Some of the people we believed in turned out to be wrong about what they thought they were doing. Some of our victories became, twenty years later, the structures we had to dismantle. The work is not a straight line. The work is sometimes a circle. You will have to learn to do it without certainty, the way your great-grandfather had to learn to do it without certainty, the way I had to.

These are the gifts I cannot give you. I am sorry for that. They are the price of having lived in a country and not in a fairy tale.

* * *

There will be days when you cannot remember why you are doing any of this. The asamblea will feel like a chore. The neighbor will be impossible. The work will seem small, and the world large, and you will wonder if it would not be easier to leave it to someone else.

When that happens, I want you to do two things.

**The first is to go and find someone older than you who has been doing the work longer than you, and ask them how they keep going.** They will tell you. They have been waiting to be asked. Some of the most important conversations in my life happened on benches with old men who had been at this longer than I had, and who had no one to tell their stories to until I sat down. You will be one of those old men, in your time. Sit on benches. Be available. The country runs on these conversations.

**The second is to read something out loud.** There were words your great-grandfather and I used to say at the start of every asamblea, when the work was new and we were not sure we could carry it. They are not magic. But they are what we said when we needed to remember why we were there. I am writing them down for you, in case you need them. Say them out loud. Say them with someone, if you can. Or alone, if you must. The body remembers what the mind forgets.

Here are the words.

> *We do not need a president. We do not need them to choose one for us. We never needed the costumes that were placed on our shoulders.*
>
> *The thing they called power was always a permission. The permission was ours from the beginning. We took it back not by seizing anything, but by stopping the giving.*
>
> *The architecture is ours. We did not inherit it. We built it. Where it falls short, we will build it again, in better forms. Where it stands, we will steward it for those not yet born.*
>
> *The tools were not given. We built them — the protocols and the archives, the cathedrals of the civic age — in the years when the choice was to build them or to be built against. We chose to build, in time.*
>
> *The source is us. It was always us. Every regime that ruled us ruled because we agreed. Every regime that ended ended because we stopped agreeing. The agreement and the ending are the only true sovereigns.*
>
> *The beauty is the fuel. We did not come this far on argument. We came this far because we made things together that we loved enough to keep making. The making is the country. The making is the politics. The making is the prayer.*
>
> *The work is not heroic. It is daily. It is small. It is the meal cooked, the neighbor greeted, the no said, the asamblea attended, the child taught, the song sung, the table set, the night sky watched.*
>
> *We are the grey men and the grey women. We were the source the whole time. We are still the source. Welcome to the work. It has been waiting for you, and it is yours.*
>
> *Que así sea.*

That is what I would tell you, if I could be there when you needed it. I cannot. So I am writing it down. Read it when you are tired. Read it to your children when they are tired. Pass it on, when you are old enough that the words will sound like wisdom in your mouth instead of borrowing.

* * *

I am almost finished.

I have written this letter at the kitchen table in your grandmother's house — the same table where, decades ago, my brother-friend wrote his essays on a borrowed laptop, the same table where, more recently, we have been gathering with the neighbors on Saturdays to hear how their lives are going. Your aunt is in the next room. Your mother, when she comes to read this to you, will find tea in the same drawer where I always kept it, behind the linen napkins your great-grandmother embroidered before I was born.

Outside, it is autumn. It is an autumn like the autumns I remember from when I was your age, and your great-grandfather was alive, and my own grandmother was still in the house in Mendoza. The light at five in the afternoon is the same light. I want you to know that. The country has changed, but the light has not, and the light is what you will remember when you remember being a child here.

There is a song I want you to learn. Mercedes Sosa sang it before I was born and your grandmother sang it to your aunt when she was small and someone, by the time you read this, will have sung it to you. *Gracias a la vida que me ha dado tanto.* Learn it. Sing it badly, the way I sang it to my daughter, until it is yours. Songs are how the country remembers itself. The country has been singing through us all along. You will sing through it, in your turn, and then your daughter will, and so on into the dark beyond what any of us can see.

I want to say one more thing, because it is what I most want you to hear.

You will be told, sometimes, that what we did was a heroic generation, that we built a country, that you owe us. *Do not believe this.* We did what we did because we had to. Most of us were tired, and afraid, and small, and we kept going because the alternative was to keep going without doing anything, which is worse. Your generation will be just as tired and afraid and small. You will do the work that comes to you because you have to, and someone will write you letters like this, and you will not feel like a hero. Good. Heroes are dangerous. The country needs grey men and grey women, not heroes. We were grey. So are you. So was every generation before us, even the ones that history named in capital letters. The capital letters are an invention of historians who needed to sell books. *The country was always built by people in lowercase.*

So when you read this, do not feel obligated. Feel addressed. The same address that was offered to me, on a bus, in 2026, when I was thinking about my daughter's school shoes. The same address that was offered to your great-grandfather, in his way. The same address that has been waiting in every Argentine kitchen for as long as Argentines have been kitchens. Someone is talking to you. They have been talking to you all your life. The talking is the country. The country is the talking.

You are the source, my love. You always were. You have nothing to prove and everything to do, and the doing will fill the days you are given.

Pick up your hand. Look at it. Now close your eyes for a second, and imagine. Your hand and your imagination, together, are the most powerful instruments of self-government any human being has ever held, and they have been yours the entire time. The hand alone repairs a chair; the imagination alone dreams a country. The pair is what is needed. One without the other is half a republic.

Now go and use them.

Your grandfather, who loved you before you were born, who is loving you while writing this, and who, by the time you read it, will be loving you from wherever the dead love their grandchildren from.

*Que así sea.*

## Cartografía

This is the closing of the arc. The references that follow are the antechamber the letter speaks from inside — the platform, the documents, and the six essays that were the scaffolding of what the letter calls *the building*.

**On the platform.**

- *Manifiesto* — the foundational declaration. Read it the moment you finish the letter; it is the document the kitchen of 2026 was built around.
- *El Mandato Vivo* — the operational instance of the citizen-governance the letter describes from the future ("the asamblea meets every fifteen days..."). What the letter remembers as ordinary, the platform is now beginning.
- *La Semilla de ¡BASTA!* — the entry point for the citizens who finish the letter and want to know how to begin. The first kitchen, made enterable.
- *Cancionero ¡BASTA!* — the songs the letter names. *Gracias a la vida* is sung in its closing pages; the cumbia, the rock nacional, the tango all live here.

**Defining documents.**

- *Perfil del Hombre Gris* — the figure the letter is written from inside. The full description of who is speaking.
- *Persona del Hombre Gris* — the voice the letter inherits. The grandfather's register, applied across the project.

**The series so far.**

There were six essays in a box of papers I left for you. They were written by a man who could see something he could not yet name. Read them as the scaffolding of the building you are reading now.

- *01 — Why Presidents Are a Stupid Idea* — that the inheritance was a king in the attic.
- *02 — The Fallacy of Democracy* — that voting was a costume.
- *03 — The Fallacy of Power Itself* — that power itself was a story we kept telling.
- *04 — An Architecture for the Grey Man* — what could replace the throne, layer by layer.
- *05 — The Sovereignty of the Grey Man* — what it meant to take the throne back, in the body.
- *06 — Beauty as Political Act* — what would carry the republic across generations.
